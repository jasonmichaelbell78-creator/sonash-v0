## Key Takeaways for the User

### 1. No claims are REFUTED. The research is fundamentally sound.

The 17-agent investigation produced a defensible schedule. All hard
dependencies, file overlaps, and effort estimates are grounded in filesystem
evidence. The WEAKENED claims are nuance issues, not foundational errors.

### 2. Five claims deserve additional scrutiny:

**a. "Repo-cleanup benefits 5 plans" (C-009) -- WEAKENED.** The benefits are
real but marginal (15-30 min total). The recommendation to run it first still
holds (zero-risk, fast), but the framing overstates urgency. Repo-cleanup is a
nice-to-have first step, not a prerequisite.

**b. "Trivially solved" (C-013) -- WEAKENED.** The scheduling algorithm is
trivial; the execution coordination is not. The report contains 5,000+ words of
careful file-conflict resolution that belies the "trivial" label. Recommend:
keep the algorithmic observation but drop the "trivially solved" framing in
favor of "the dependency structure is simple, but the shared-resource
coordination requires care."

**c. "PS before SWS saves 1-2 sessions" (C-012) -- WEAKENED.** The directional
claim is correct, but the magnitude is speculative. SWS's 16-item maturity
checklist may not even surface Guardrail #6 behavioral violations. Savings could
be 0.5-2 sessions. The recommendation (PS before SWS) still holds regardless.

**d. "Propagation resolves DEBT-2121" (C-007) -- WEAKENED.** The write-path
centralization in Step 9 may not address the execSync command injection vector.
This needs verification during execution -- the command injection is in how the
script processes items (execSync), not in how it writes MASTER_DEBT.jsonl. The
DEBT-9295 resolution (CI non-blocking via Step 2) is solid.

**e. "Total 86-139 sessions" (C-024) -- WEAKENED.** This is accurate for the
7-plan scope but provides no conditional scenario for the Meta Pipeline
enforcement case. Additionally, cli-tools is likely 3-4 sessions (not 2) given
its multi-locale installation and XL header in its own PLAN.md. These do not
materially change the total (SWS dominates), but should be noted.

### 3. The biggest risk the report correctly identifies but perhaps underweights:

The Meta Pipeline question (Open Question #1) is not just an "open question" --
it is potentially a schedule-blocking decision. If the ROADMAP's T&I -> CQ -> DE
-> SWS sequence is still enforced, the entire schedule shifts by potentially
20-40+ sessions. The report flags this but treats it as one of 5 open questions
rather than THE critical decision point. **Recommend: elevate Open Question #1
to a blocking decision that the user must resolve before Wave 2 (SWS start).**

### 4. The agent-env -> SWS dependency is more nuanced than "all 5 phases":

The STEP_3_14_COVERAGE_AUDIT.md shows that agent-env only PARTIALLY covers SWS
Step 13 requirements (3 of 6 fully covered, 3 partially). Specifically, Zod
schemas, lifecycle deprecation, and inter-ecosystem contracts are partially
covered. This means that even after agent-env Phase 5 completes, SWS Step 13
still has significant work to do. The hard dependency is real (agent-env
produces the agent definitions SWS would standardize), but the claim that
"agent-env subsumes SWS Step 3.14" overstates the coverage. Agent-env provides
~75% of the foundation; SWS Step 13 adds the remaining structural
standardization (Zod schemas, formal contracts, deprecation policies).
