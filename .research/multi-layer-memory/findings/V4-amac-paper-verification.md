## 2. Claim-by-Claim Verification

### C-032: "A-MAC found 'content type prior' is the strongest single predictor"

**Research claim:** "A-MAC (March 2026) found that 'content type prior' is the
strongest single predictor of whether a memory entry should be stored --
stronger than recency, semantic novelty, or utility." (Confidence: HIGH, Source:
D4a)

**Paper says:** Ablation study (Table 2) shows removing Type Prior causes the
largest F1 drop: deltaF1 = -0.107, reducing F1 from 0.583 to 0.476. This is
larger than the drop from removing any of the other four factors.

**Verdict: VERIFIED.** The paper's ablation study does identify content type
prior as the most influential of the five factors. The research claim accurately
represents this finding.

---

### C-120: "Content type prior means whitelist of categories is most effective admission gate"

**Research claim:** "The A-MAC academic finding on 'content type prior' means
the most effective memory admission gate is a whitelist of category types rather
than a scoring formula. Categories: user-corrections, architecture-decisions,
hook-patterns, recurring-errors." (Confidence: MEDIUM, Source: D6b)

**Paper says:** Type Prior assigns "higher priors to stable information (e.g.,
preferences or identity statements) and lower priors to transient states" using
"rule-based pattern matching with part-of-speech cues." The paper does NOT
prescribe a specific whitelist, and it does NOT recommend type prior as a
standalone gate -- it is one of five factors in a composite scoring system.

**Verdict: OVERSTATED.** The research makes three inferential leaps:

1. The paper uses type prior as ONE factor in a composite score, not as a
   standalone whitelist gate
2. The specific categories (user-corrections, architecture-decisions, etc.) are
   the research agent's invention, not from the paper
3. A-MAC's Type Prior uses continuous scoring with rule-based pattern matching,
   not a binary whitelist

The directional insight (type-based filtering is valuable) is correct. The
specific implementation recommendation (whitelist gate) is the research agent's
design, attributed to the paper.

### C-215: "Content-type-prior filter eliminates approximately 70% of low-signal memory write candidates"

**Research claim:** "The content-type-prior filter (A-MAC paper) eliminates
approximately 70% of low-signal memory write candidates and is the single
highest-leverage memory quality improvement available at near-zero cost."
(Confidence: HIGH, Sources: D8b-1, D4a)

**Paper says:** No percentage for candidate elimination appears anywhere in the
paper. The paper reports:

- F1 = 0.583 on LoCoMo benchmark
- 31% latency reduction vs A-mem baseline
- deltaF1 = -0.107 when Type Prior is ablated
- Benchmark: ~1,500 candidates across 30 conversations

The "70% elimination" figure does not appear in the paper.

**Verdict: FABRICATED FIGURE.** The 70% number was generated by the research
agent (D8b-1 synthesizing D4a) and then attributed to the paper. The research's
own contrarian review (Contrarian #8) caught this and recommended downgrading to
MEDIUM, but the claims.jsonl still records it as HIGH confidence.

Note: V3 verification also caught this: "The '70%' elimination figure is NOT
from the paper -- it is an agent estimate."

### C-229: "Existing MEMORY.md taxonomy maps directly to A-MAC content-type-prior categories"

**Research claim:** "The existing MEMORY.md taxonomy (user*\*, feedback*\_,
project\_\_, reference\_\*) maps directly to the A-MAC content-type-prior
categories. The admission filter is already implicitly implemented; it needs to
be made explicit and enforced." (Confidence: HIGH, Sources: D9a, D8b-1)

**Paper says:** A-MAC's Type Prior uses "preferences or identity statements"
(high prior) vs "transient states" (low prior) as examples. The paper provides
no taxonomy that maps to MEMORY.md's prefix convention.

**Verdict: OVERSTATED.** The MEMORY.md taxonomy is a valid content-type system,
and the concept of content-type-based filtering is supported by A-MAC. But the
claim that MEMORY.md's taxonomy "maps directly to A-MAC categories" is false --
A-MAC does not define the categories the research describes. The mapping is the
research agent's inference, not a paper finding.

## 3. Summary of Findings

| Claim | Verdict           | Severity                                      |

### What the paper actually says (ground truth)

1. Memory value can be decomposed into 5 factors: Future Utility, Factual
   Confidence, Semantic Novelty, Temporal Recency, and Content Type Prior
2. Content Type Prior has the largest ablation impact (deltaF1 = -0.107)
3. A-MAC achieves F1 = 0.583 on LoCoMo, 31% lower latency than A-mem
4. Type Prior uses rule-based pattern matching with POS cues to assign
   continuous scores, not binary whitelist decisions
5. Benchmark: 30 conversations, 15-40 turns each, ~1,500 candidates

### What the research added (agent inference, not paper content)

1. The "70% elimination" figure (fabricated, no paper source)
2. The specific whitelist categories (user-corrections, architecture-decisions,
   hook-patterns, recurring-errors)
3. The recommendation to use Type Prior as a standalone gate rather than one
   factor in a composite score
4. The CLAUDE.md admission policy design
5. The claim that MEMORY.md taxonomy "maps directly" to A-MAC categories

## 4. Risk Assessment

**Impact on research validity:** MODERATE. The core insight -- that content-type
filtering is the highest-leverage component of memory admission -- is genuinely
supported by the paper. The research recommendations (admission policy
whitelist, MEMORY.md formalization) are reasonable design choices. The problem
is attribution: presenting agent-generated design decisions as paper findings
inflates confidence and makes the recommendations harder to evaluate on their
own merits.

**Impact on dependent claims:** C-215's fabricated 70% figure cascades into
C-216's ROI calculation, which was used to rank the admission policy as the #1
recommended action. Without the 70% figure, the recommendation still has merit
but the quantified ROI is unsupported.

**Paper credibility:** The paper itself is legitimate but carries risk factors:

- Workshop paper (not main conference track)
- Zero citations (27 days old, so this is expected but means no independent
  validation)
- Single benchmark (LoCoMo only)
- 8 authors, no major institutional affiliations visible

## 5. Recommended Corrections

1. **C-215:** Change confidence from HIGH to LOW. Remove "approximately 70%"
   phrasing entirely. Replace with: "A-MAC's ablation study shows content-type
   prior is the most influential admission factor (deltaF1 = -0.107). The
   elimination rate for a whitelist implementation is unknown and untested."

2. **C-120:** Add note that the specific categories are agent-designed, not
   paper-prescribed. The whitelist-vs-composite-score design choice should be
   presented as a simplification decision, not a paper recommendation.

3. **C-229:** Remove "maps directly to" language. Replace with "is conceptually
   aligned with" -- the mapping is reasonable but not paper-endorsed.

4. **C-216:** Decouple from the 70% figure. The recommendation to add an
   admission policy to CLAUDE.md stands on its own merits (low cost, no
   infrastructure) without needing a fabricated elimination rate to justify it.

5. **C-032:** No change needed. This claim accurately represents the paper.
